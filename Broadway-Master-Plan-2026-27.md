## Our four content pillars

Every piece of content — blog, Instagram, email, poster — is anchored to one of these four pillars. When in doubt about whether to make something, ask which pillar it serves. If it doesn't serve any of them, don't make it.

| Pillar | Line | What it means in practice |
|--------|------|--------------------------|
| 1 | **Not every bottle, the right one** | Curation over volume. We push back on "biggest selection" competitors by being the shop that knows what's on the shelf and why. |
| 2 | **Without the pretension** | Wine and spirits knowledge delivered plainly. No gatekeeping. The Baudry post is the voice reference. |
| 3 | **Kitsilano since 1986** | Neighbourhood first. BC producers first. 40 years on this block earns opinions. |
| 4 | **Good bottles are meant to be shared** | Gifting, events, loyalty, community. The social side of what we do. |

## Part 8: Blog Content Calendar 2026

### How the blog fits the system

The blog is the SEO foundation and the long-form home for everything else. A blog post takes 30 to 60 minutes to write. An Instagram carousel takes 20. An email takes 15. The blog post is the raw material; everything else is a cut of it.

Every post should:
- Target a specific search phrase a Vancouver buyer would actually type
- Be written in Chris's voice (see brand-voice rules in the Playbook Part 4)
- Have a soft CTA at the end, never a hard sell
- Spin off at minimum one Instagram caption and one email mention
- No em dashes, ever

**Publishing rhythm:** roughly bi-weekly, timed to anchor moments or seasonal turns. Pair with an Instagram carousel or Reel the same week.

## Part 9: In-Store Poster and Physical Channel

### Why this matters

The playbook covers every digital channel. But 100 percent of our customers walk into a physical space. The in-store environment is a channel. It is also one of the few channels where we have zero competition for attention — the customer is standing in front of us.

The physical channel has five formats:

| Format | Placement | Lead time | Who produces | Notes |
|--------|-----------|-----------|--------------|-------|
| **Poster** | In-store walls / entrance / near relevant section | 1 week | Chris + Canva → local print | A3 or A2. Tier 1 events only. No window — use internal wall space or entrance area. |
| **Till screen graphic** | POS monitor at checkout | Same day | Chris + Canva → USB or direct load | 16:9 landscape. Customer is captive while paying. Zero print cost. Update for every Tier 1 + Tier 2 anchor. |
| **Shelf cards** | Individual bottle/section | 2 days | Chris + Canva or hand-written | Tier 1 + 2. |
| **Counter card** | Point of sale counter | 1 week | Chris + Canva | A5 tent card. |
| **A-frame sidewalk sign** | Outside entrance | Same day | Dry-erase or printed insert | All tiers. |

### In-store channel rules

1. **Posters are your in-store billboard.** One message, one product, one reason to look twice. Design in Canva, print locally. Mount near the relevant section or at the entrance for Tier 1 events.
2. **The till screen is your most captive audience.** The customer is standing still, waiting to pay. That is the moment for a loyalty sign-up prompt, an event teaser, or a product they just walked past. Update for every Tier 1 and Tier 2 anchor. Design in Canva — 16:9 landscape — zero print cost.
3. **Shelf cards earn the sale.** The customer is standing in front of the bottle. A 20-word shelf card that says what it tastes like and who it's for converts more than a price tag alone.
4. **The A-frame is for same-day impulse.** Update it at open. When the Canucks play at 7 PM, the A-frame at 5 PM should say something about it.
5. **Don't activate everything for every event.** Tier 1 anchors get the full set: poster + till + shelf + counter. Tier 2: till + shelf cards only. Tier 3: nothing physical. Restraint is what makes the Tier 1 moments land.
6. **Canva templates for everything.** Same brand fonts, same colour palette as digital. Build a poster template and a till screen template — lock them and reuse across the year.

## Part 11: Blog voice rules (canonical reference)

These rules govern every blog post. They are non-negotiable. Cowork references these. Chris enforces them on review.

**Voice reference post:** Bernard Baudry: The Real Deal in Chinon (February 2, 2026)
URL: calliope-duck-4dtd.squarespace.com/blog — read this before drafting anything.

**The rules:**

1. **No em dashes. Ever.** Use a comma, a period, or rewrite the sentence. This is the single most important rule.
2. **"We" voice throughout.** Never "I". Never third person. We are the shop, speaking as one.
3. **Short paragraphs.** Two to four sentences max. White space is not wasted space.
4. **Bold headers, plainly named.** Not clever. "What it tastes like." "Why we stock it." "What to pair it with." The reader should be able to scan and land.
5. **Specific over general.** Name the producer. Name the vintage. Name the price. "A nice Okanagan Pinot" is not a sentence. "Blue Mountain Pinot Noir 2022 at $34.99" is.
6. **State opinions plainly.** "This is the bottle we push constantly and we will not apologize for it." Not "some people prefer" or "it could be argued." We know things. We say them.
7. **Soft CTA at the end.** Never a hard sell. "Come find it on the shelf" or "it's here if you want it." Never "buy now" or "limited time."
8. **No therapeutic claims.** BC LCRB compliance. Wine does not cure, heal, restore, or improve health.
9. **No negative comparisons.** Don't name competitors. Don't say what we're not. Say what we are.
10. **Vancouver-specific references always preferred.** "A Tuesday night at home in Kitsilano" beats "a quiet evening." Ground the writing in the neighbourhood.

## Part 13: Direct Mail and Postcard Channel

### Why direct mail still works for a shop like ours

Our loyalty customers are not all on Instagram. Some of them have been shopping here since the 1990s. A physical card in the hand — their name on it, a real reason to come in — does things that a Stories post cannot. Direct mail for small local retail has seen a measurable comeback in Canada post-2022 because inbox saturation has made physical mail feel premium again. We are a premium shop. The channel fits.

The cadence is bi-annual: twice a year, sent to all active Grand Crew loyalty members plus any customer who joined the program in the preceding 90 days. This keeps the list fresh, rewards recent sign-ups, and gives us two moments per year to re-engage people who have gone quiet.

### Direct mail channel rules

1. **Personalise it.** Name on the card. Not "Dear Valued Customer." If the list tool can't personalise, rethink the list tool.
2. **One offer, one action.** The card should ask for exactly one thing. Enter the contest. Or come in for a special. Not both.
3. **Plain language on the mechanics.** If someone has to read it twice to understand how to enter, rewrite it.
4. **BC contest regulations apply.** No purchase necessary to enter is mandatory language. Confirm with a brief legal review before each send.
5. **Photograph everything.** The draw, the winner pickup, the card design, the print run. All of it is Instagram and email content.
6. **Tie the send to a moment.** June because summer opens. November because holidays open. Don't send postcards in quiet months where there is no reason to act now.
